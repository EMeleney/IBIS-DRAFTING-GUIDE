Note Guidance / reference
1
Review intake materials. Thoroughly read the client questionnaire and intake notes. Confirm marital status, family structure, and whether this is a new trust or restatement. Pay attention to minor children, unique asset types (real estate, businesses), and special planning concerns. Retrieve and review existing trust documents for restatements; note whether the trust date should be preserved.
2
Launch WealthCounsel. Select client profile and input full names (double-check spelling), contact info and mailing address (used for Certification of Trust), family members in birth order using dropdowns for relationships. Choose correct scenario template (e.g., married with adult children). Enter seed funding amount (e.g., $10).
3
Draft core trust provisions. Trust name and date. Initial and successor trustees (include addresses). Confirm SSN will be used during life.
4
Define distribution terms. Choose strategy: outright at specific ages (e.g., 25/30/35), continue in trust with discretionary distributions, or conservative guidelines for minors. Build in support for mental health, addiction, or educational needs if applicable.
5
Contingencies and beneficiary clauses. Include alternate plans for common disaster scenarios. Default to heirs-at-law or prompt client to name specific people/charities. Confirm use of specific vs. general descendant clauses (e.g., naming children vs. "my descendants"). For clients with no children, remove all references to "Children" and "Descendants."
6
Trustee structure and governance. Name independent and successor trustees. Determine if Independent Special Trustee is necessary; remove if not required and revise cross-references (Sections 9(c), 10.07(g)). Address resignation, incapacity (2-doctor rule), and removal provisions.
7
Additional articles and customizations. Review and include: retirement plans language (especially if trust is TOD on IRAs/401ks), specific gifts or occupancy rights, Medicaid planning or tax election language, spendthrift protections, step-up in basis, discretionary distribution controls. Avoid unnecessary complexity — remove trustee-owned life insurance provisions unless creating an ILIT.
8
Download, edit, and tailor documents. Apply all post-WealthCounsel customizations per this guide. Update all fields in WealthCounsel Ribbon. Run Ctrl+F for unresolved error codes: "Error! Bookmark not defined" and "UNANSWERED." Update table of contents only after all drafting and editing is complete.
General Principle
Note WealthCounsel templates are boilerplate-heavy — cut accordingly

WealthCounsel templates are long and often boilerplate-heavy. Cut unnecessary provisions to improve clarity and reduce client overwhelm. Clients consistently respond better to documents that are short, clearly written, and tailored to their specific circumstances. Do not include language simply because it is in the template. Always evaluate what each provision accomplishes and whether it is relevant to the client's goals. If you are unsure about a provision, proposed edit, or planning approach — ask. No two plans are alike, and thoughtful collaboration improves the end result for both the client and the firm.

Before drafting: Double-check the Fee Agreement to confirm you are drafting the documents listed in it — not adding unnecessary documents.

Format Font · Address in preamble (Lake & Geauga County clients)

Use Georgia font. For clients residing in Lake County and Geauga County, INCLUDE their full residential address in the preamble. All other counties — omit.

When to use: Second marriage, meaningful divorce/creditor risk, large inheritance, or closely held business interests — when grantor wants added guardrails against spousal pressure. Pair with an independent distribution decision-maker and a preference for direct payments to providers. The clause alone will not prevent commingling after distributions.

Notwithstanding any other provision of this trust, a beneficiary shall not serve as the sole Trustee of the trust created for his or her benefit unless and until such beneficiary, if married or contemplating marriage, has obtained a signed agreement from his or her spouse or fiancé(e), as applicable, irrevocably waiving, disclaiming, and relinquishing any and all rights, title, or claims, whether statutory, equitable, or otherwise, to the property of the trust, including but not limited to any claims arising under community property, equitable distribution, marital property, or similar laws. Situational Foreign trustee risk — Option A (hard prohibition) or Option B (preference with safeguards)

If there is meaningful risk that a successor trustee may reside outside the U.S., include to avoid negative tax consequences under the "foreign trust rules" of the IRC. Trust must qualify as a U.S. Person under IRC § 7701(a)(30)(E).

Option A — Hard prohibition:

No Foreign Trustee Permitted to Serve If the Trustee I nominate to serve is a foreign national or a citizen of the United States who is not residing in the United States, my Trust may be subject to the "foreign trust rules" of the Internal Revenue Code. I intend that all trusts created by this instrument qualify as "United States Persons" under IRC Section 7701(a)(30)(E), and one or more United States Persons shall always have the authority to control all substantial decisions under this instrument. A person who is not a "United States Person" [as defined in IRC Section 7701(a)(30)(A) through (C)] shall not serve as a Trustee or Trust Protector, and any power, fiduciary or otherwise, that a person who is not a United States Person may hold shall be effective only to the extent such power is not the power to make a "substantial decision," as defined in Treasury Regulation Section 301.7701-7, or elsewhere. Furthermore, any person who does not reside in one of the fifty (50) states of the United States shall be disqualified from serving as Trustee or Trust Protector of any trust created under this instrument, and the nomination shall pass to the next-named Trustee who is not a foreign national.

Option B — Preference with structural safeguards:

United States Trust Status It is my intent that all trusts created by this instrument qualify as "United States Persons" under IRC Section 7701(a)(30)(E), and that one or more "United States Persons" (as defined in IRC Section 7701(a)(30)(A) through (C)) at all times have the authority to control all "substantial decisions" within the meaning of Treasury Regulation Section 301.7701-7 (or any successor provision). It is my strong preference that no person who is not a United States Person serve as Trustee or Trust Protector of any trust created under this instrument. It is also my strong preference that any Trustee or Trust Protector reside in one of the fifty (50) states of the United States. Notwithstanding these preferences, the appointment or service of a person who is not a United States Person, or who does not reside in one of the fifty (50) states, shall not be invalid solely by reason of that status or residence; however, any power, whether fiduciary or otherwise, held by such person shall be exercisable only to the extent that (i) the exercise of the power would not constitute control of any "substantial decision," and (ii) one or more United States Persons retain exclusive authority to control all substantial decisions. To the extent necessary to carry out this intent, any provision of this instrument shall be construed to allocate and/or limit powers so that the substantial decisions are controlled by one or more United States Persons. In making any appointment to fill a vacancy or to name a successor Trustee or Trust Protector, the nominating person(s) shall give priority to candidates who are United States Persons and residents of one of the fifty (50) states, consistent with the foregoing intent and preferences.

Article: Descendants' Trusts
Format Use children's names — not generic "Descendants" language

In the Division of Trust Property section, use kids' names instead of general "Descendant's" language. If only one child, DELETE paragraphs and ADD:

The Trustee shall hold and administer my remaining trust property for my [RELATION], [CHILD'S NAME], per stirpes, as provided in the next Section.
Remove If adult children become immediate trustee of own trust — delete discretionary distribution and withdrawal sections

If adult child beneficiaries will immediately become trustee of their own trust, DELETE the "Guidelines for Discretionary Distributions" section and the "Right to Withdraw Principal" section.

Note General Needs Trust with mandatory 5% distribution — use Unitrust Amount structure

Do not use the standard "discretionary income and principal" distribution combined with a "5 or 5 power" at Trust Termination. That approach produces technically similar tax results but creates overly complex IRS-speak withdrawal language.

Instead: Start with a General Needs Trust. After the screen regarding whether descendants are beneficiaries, select the options that generate a Unitrust Amount distribution structure. This produces language requiring the Trustee to distribute a fixed annual percentage (typically 5%) directly to the beneficiary, with additional discretionary distributions permitted if appropriate.

Article: Remote Contingent Beneficiaries
Remove Remove article when many multi-generational beneficiaries — update Trust Summary article references

Remove when there are many beneficiaries who can take, particularly if they are of different generations — it is very unlikely nobody will take, and the default mirrors Ohio law anyway. If removing, update Trust Summary article references.

When the Simplified Version of the WealthCounsel Trust omits important language needed for complex or personalized scenarios, locate provisions from either: (1) the Full-Length Trust in WealthCounsel, or (2) a prior trust drafted for a client with a similar fact pattern.

To access the Full-Length Trust: return to the beginning of the interview, select "Yes" when prompted to "Change the Trust Length," then choose "Full" and answer all applicable interview questions.
